## III. THE STATISTICAL ANOMALY: BELFAST ICC DIAGNOSTIC OUTPUT

|---|---|
| Total children diagnosed | **216** |
| Long QT Syndrome | 190 (88%) |
| Brugada Syndrome | 22 (10%) |
| CPVT | 4 (2%) |
| Diagnosed via family screening | **95%** |
| SCDs found only at molecular autopsy (unknown to clinic) | **5** |

The 95% cascade screening rate means virtually every child was diagnosed not because they presented with symptoms, but because a family member was diagnosed first. The entire diagnostic pyramid rests on the integrity of the index diagnoses. If those are fabricated, 95% of children may carry false diagnoses.

The study's own conclusion undermines any claim of geographic genetic clustering: the genetic profile is "broadly similar to that reported worldwide." The diagnoses are concentrated geographically. The genetics are not.

### The Albinni Paradox: Proof That Screening Inflates LQTS But Not BrS

A second 2025 pediatric channelopathy study provides an independent control comparison that exposes the anomaly from a different angle.

**Albinni et al. (Austria, *European Journal of Pediatrics* 2025)** studied pediatric channelopathies under "real world" conditions with no systematic screening program. Comparing the two:

| Condition | Kendall (NI, active screening + Ajmaline) | Albinni (Austria, no screening) | Ratio |
|---|---|---|---|
| LQTS prevalence | 1 in 2,500 | 1 in 13,000 | **5.2x higher in NI** |
| BrS prevalence | 1 in 22,000 | 1 in 22,000 | **Identical** |

LQTS prevalence is **5.2 times higher** in Northern Ireland than Austria. This is the expected screening effect: active cascade screening finds more cases. The LQTS ratio is the control confirmation that Kendall's screening program inflates diagnosis rates, exactly as methodology would predict.

**So why does the screening effect not appear for BrS?**

Active cascade screening inflates LQTS prevalence by 5.2x relative to passive ascertainment. Ajmaline provocation (used in 80% of Kendall's BrS diagnoses) should inflate BrS prevalence further. Yet the BrS prevalence is identical between active screening (NI) and no screening (Austria). Either there is a biological explanation (an undiscovered age-dependent ceiling on pediatric BrS prevalence that operates identically across all populations and methods) or the BrS numbers are being managed. ## IV. THREE ERRORS, ONE DIRECTION: THE KENDALL PAPER

Independent forensic review of the full Kendall et al. paper confirmed three compounding errors within a single sentence of the Discussion section, all operating in the same direction.

### The Phantom Citation

Kendall states: *"the estimated prevalence of 1 in 22,000 is similar to a previous study prospectively screening children in Japan, which only found 1 in 20,000 [10]."*

The study being described is Yamakawa et al. (2004). **Yamakawa does not appear anywhere in Kendall's 28-reference bibliography.** Reference [10] is Landrum et al. (2014), the ClinVar genomic database paper. It has nothing to do with Japanese pediatric screening. The citation is a phantom.

### The Wrong Comparator Figure

The "1 in 20,000" figure is incorrect. Yamakawa et al. screened 20,387 Japanese schoolchildren and identified 2 with full Brugada-type ECG conformity: a prevalence of **1 in 10,194, not "1 in 20,000."** The figure has been approximately doubled. This doubling inflates any overdiagnosis calculation that uses Kendall's figure as its starting point.

|-----------|----------------------|----------------------|
| **Population** | Unselected Japanese schoolchildren | Pre-selected high-risk families (95% cascade) |

| **What was detected** | Spontaneous Type 1 Brugada pattern | Drug-unmasked Brugada pattern (80%) |
| **Sensitivity** | Lowest (resting ECG alone) | Highest (pharmacological provocation) |
