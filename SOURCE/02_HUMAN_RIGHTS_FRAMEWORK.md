## II. TORTURE, 18 U.S.C. §§ 2340–2340B

### The Statute

Section 2340A makes it a federal crime for any person to commit or attempt to commit torture outside the United States, where the alleged offender is a U.S. national or is present in the United States.

Section 2340 defines torture as an act committed by a person acting under the **color of law** specifically intended to inflict **severe physical or mental pain or suffering** upon another person within his custody or physical control.

**Statutory requirement:** The perpetrator must act under the color of law, i.e., in an official or quasi-official capacity.

**Evidence:**

The physicians, nurses, and technicians operating the cardiac screening program act under state authority.

- **MI5 permanent operational control:** The St Andrews Agreement, Annex E (2006), transferred permanent control of national security intelligence in Northern Ireland to MI5. This was implemented during a period of direct rule when the Northern Ireland Assembly (Stormont) was prorogued between 2002 and 2007. MI5's accountability runs to Westminster, not to the devolved Northern Ireland government, not to the Police Service of Northern Ireland, and not to the Northern Ireland Policing Board.

- **The Special Reconnaissance Regiment (SRR):** Annex E also established the operational framework under which British Army special forces, specifically the SRR, operate under MI5 direction in Northern Ireland. The Committee on the Administration of Justice (CAJ) described this arrangement as a **"parallel police force"** with **"minimal or non-existent"** accountability to devolved NI institutions. ## III. WAR CRIMES, 18 U.S.C. § 2441

### The Statute

Section 2441 makes it a federal crime for a U.S. national or member of the U.S. Armed Forces, or any person with respect to a victim who is a U.S. national or Armed Forces member, to commit a war crime, defined to include grave breaches of the Geneva Conventions and other specified violations.

### The Armed Conflict Threshold

Section 2441 requires that the conduct occur in the context of and in association with an **armed conflict**. This is the threshold question.

Northern Ireland is not currently in a state of armed conflict as conventionally understood. The Good Friday Agreement (1998) ended the formal conflict, and no international or non-international armed conflict is recognized by international bodies.

However, three considerations warrant preserving this statutory framework in the analysis:

1. **The St Andrews Agreement (2006) preserved a wartime intelligence architecture.** Annex E transferred permanent MI5 control of national security intelligence in Northern Ireland during a period of direct rule, when the Northern Ireland Assembly (Stormont) was prorogued between 2002 and 2007. This arrangement was designed for counter-terrorism during the Troubles. The continuation of this wartime architecture into peacetime, including the SRR operating as a parallel policing structure with no accountability to devolved institutions, and its apparent deployment against a civilian population for resource extraction purposes, raises the question of whether the legal framework governing intelligence operations in Northern Ireland has itself failed to transition to peacetime.

2. **Common Article 3 of the Geneva Conventions** applies to situations of internal armed conflict and prohibits violence to life and person, cruel treatment, torture, and outrages upon personal dignity against persons taking no active part in hostilities. If the intelligence architecture established during the Troubles is being used to target civilians during peacetime, there is an argument, not yet established but worth investigating, that the state itself has not ended the conflict with respect to this population.

3. **The Military Commissions Act of 2006** amended § 2441 to include specific offenses, murder, torture, cruel or inhumane treatment, taking hostages, intentionally causing great suffering, that do not require a full armed conflict determination if committed by or against U.S. nationals.

### Applicable Provisions (If Armed Conflict Threshold Met)

- **§ 2441(d)(1)(A), Torture:** Identical in substance to the § 2340 analysis above

- **§ 2441(d)(1)(B), Cruel or inhumane treatment:** A broader standard than torture, encompassing treatment that causes serious physical or mental pain but may fall short of the "severe" threshold under § 2340

- **§ 2441(d)(1)(D), Intentionally causing great suffering or serious injury to body or health:** The unnecessary pacemaker implantation, the chronic administration of beta blockers to children, and the psychological burden of false terminal diagnoses fall within this provision

## IV. IMMIGRATION CONSEQUENCES, PERSECUTOR BAR AND DEPORTABILITY

### 8 U.S.C. § 1182(a)(3)(E), Inadmissibility

Any alien who has **ordered, incited, assisted, or otherwise participated in** persecution on account of race, religion, nationality, membership in a particular social group, or political opinion is **inadmissible** to the United States.

The persecutor bar does not require a criminal conviction. It does not require proof beyond reasonable doubt. It requires a reasonable basis to believe the individual participated in persecution based on a protected characteristic.

**Application:**

Every medical professional, intelligence operative, and government official identified in Document 07 (Perpetrator Profiles) who is not a U.S. citizen is subject to the persecutor bar if the evidence supports their participation in the systematic medical targeting of the Irish Catholic population.

The persecutor bar is particularly powerful in this context because:

- It operates independently of criminal prosecution, HRSP can refer individuals for visa revocation even if criminal proceedings are not initiated

- It captures a broad range of conduct, not just the physicians who issued the diagnoses, but administrators who facilitated the cascade screening, intelligence operatives who directed the concealment, and officials who prevented investigation

- The "persecution" standard under immigration law is lower than the statutory elements of genocide or torture, it requires discriminatory conduct causing harm on account of a protected characteristic, without the "severe pain or suffering" threshold of § 2340 or the "intent to destroy" requirement of § 1091

### 8 U.S.C. § 1227(a)(4)(D), Deportability

Any alien present in the United States who has participated in persecution under § 1182(a)(3)(E) is **deportable**.

**Application:**

If any identified perpetrator is currently present in the United States, as a visa holder, legal permanent resident, or otherwise, they are deportable upon a showing that they participated in the persecution. HRSP has coordinated denaturalization proceedings in analogous cases.
